# Referee Report — Reviewer 3 (TCP Phase Metallurgy, Senior Reviewer)

**Manuscript:** "Data-efficient machine learning of complex Fe–Mo topologically close-packed intermetallics using domain knowledge of chemistry and crystallography"  
**Report round:** 1

---

## Summary

This manuscript addresses an important and underexplored problem in computational alloy metallurgy: whether machine-learning interatomic potentials trained on a comparatively small DFT dataset can reproduce the energetics and, by extension, the phase stability of complex Fe–Mo TCP intermetallics. The paper is strong in its experimental-prediction comparison for R-phase sublattice occupancies and in its explicit acknowledgment that the Bragg–Williams/CEF approximation neglects short-range order and vibrational entropy. The introduction correctly motivates the relevance of TCP phases to Fe-based structural materials. The CNAV scheme is a novel and conceptually clear contribution that bridges the gap between crystallography-agnostic ML and the Wyckoff-site hierarchy that characterises TCP phases. Nevertheless, the manuscript has gaps in crystallographic description that will frustrate readers who want to understand the physics, and there are ambiguities in the thermodynamic analysis that need to be resolved.

---

## Major Comments

**1. Crystal structure descriptions lack space groups and Pearson symbols.** The TCP phases discussed (σ, μ, C36, R, χ, C14, C15, A15, M, P, δ) are identified only by their conventional names and atom counts. A PRB paper on TCP intermetallics must provide at minimum the space group and Pearson symbol for each phase. Readers unfamiliar with the TCP family need this information to assess the structural complexity and to reproduce the dataset. The M phase, for example, is not unambiguously identified in the manuscript — M phase in the Fe–Mo context typically refers to the Mo₆Fe₇ Laves-type structure (hP13, space group P6₃/mmc) but this should be stated explicitly. Similarly, the δ phase should be specified with its composition range, space group (Cmcm, Pearson oC8 for the MoFe type) and the reason for its exclusion from training. A table of TCP phases with space group, Pearson symbol, number of Wyckoff sites, and stability range in the Fe–Mo diagram (even if approximate) would add substantially to the scientific value of the Methods section and is standard practice in papers of this type.

**2. Inconsistency between temperature analysis and the experimental phase diagram.** The thermodynamic calculations (Fig. 4 and associated discussion) are performed at 1700 K and present compound energy formalism (CEF) results at this temperature. The equilibrium Fe–Mo phase diagram shows the R phase to be stable at approximately 1300–1700 K at the composition investigated; the 1700 K CEF calculation is therefore near the upper stability limit. The δ phase in contrast is understood to be a metastable phase in Fe–Mo, as acknowledged in the manuscript. However, the manuscript does not comment on the fact that the predicted convex-hull topology at 1700 K (whether σ, R, or other phases are stable) should be compared with the assessed binary CALPHAD Fe–Mo phase diagram, such as the assessment by Joubert and Crivello. Making this comparison — even qualitatively — is necessary to establish that the Gibbs free energy model is physically reasonable. Without it, the reader cannot determine whether the CEF-based thermodynamics is producing the correct stable phase assemblage.

---

## Minor Comments

**1. Wyckoff site labelling conventions and reproducibility.** The R-phase Wyckoff sites are referred to in the occupancy comparison (Fig. 6) using labels such as B1, B2, D1, D2, etc., which appear to come from the FeMo.csv experimental dataset. These labels do not correspond to the standard Wyckoff notation for the R-phase space group (R-3m, 159 atoms per hexagonal cell, with Wyckoff letters a, b, c, d, e, f). The manuscript should clarify whether these labels are a subset of the canonical Wyckoff sites or author-defined site identifiers, and should provide a mapping to the standard notation. Without this, crystallographers cannot connect the predicted occupancies to the structural literature.

**2. Absence of the Havinga structure-map literature.** The TCP field has a substantial tradition of structure-map approaches to phase stability prediction (Pettifor, Villars–Calvert, Zunger). While the manuscript is not a structure-map study, the Introduction and Discussion would benefit from one or two sentences acknowledging how the present data-driven approach relates to or complements these established semi-empirical frameworks. The omission of any reference to structure maps may give referees in this area the impression that the authors are unaware of this prior art. References such as Pettifor (1988, Mater. Sci. Tech.) or Villars et al. (1989) would be appropriate.

**3. R-phase composition range and training set representativeness.** The training set includes 30 R-phase structures (24 after the 80/20 split), which represents a 1.6% coverage of the full sublattice configuration space for the five-Wyckoff-site R-phase description. The manuscript presents learning-curve evidence (Fig. 2) that performance plateaus for the full descriptor set, but this plateau analysis applies to the combined training set and not specifically to R-phase structures. Given that the R phase is the primary target of the thermodynamic and occupancy analysis in the paper, a brief discussion of whether the R-phase-specific RMSE (23.2 meV/atom for BOP+CNAV, from Table 3) is statistically robust given only 24 training structures would strengthen the paper.

**4. Technological motivation — Mo content in engineering alloys.** The opening paragraph of the Introduction mentions "Fe-based steels and superalloys" as the technological motivation. The typical Mo content of Mo-containing stainless steels (316L: 2–3 wt%) and tool steels is far below the Mo-rich compositions where TCP phases form (x_Mo = 0.15–0.50 in the Fe–Mo binary). The manuscript should clarify whether the primary technological driver is (a) Mo-bearing stainless and creep-resistant steels (low Mo, but TCP precipitation at grain boundaries as a deleterious effect) or (b) high-temperature Mo-Fe binders and refractory alloys (high Mo, where TCP phases are the matrix). This distinction changes the relevant composition window and the interpretation of the training set coverage.

**5. Bragg–Williams treatment and short-range order.** The Discussion correctly notes that the Bragg–Williams/CEF formalism neglects short-range order. The manuscript would benefit from a quantitative estimate of the expected magnitude of this contribution, even if only a bound. For TCP phases with sublattice size mismatches of the order of 0.2–0.4 eV/atom per site pair (as found from the DFT data), short-range-order corrections from, for example, the quasi-chemical approximation can shift transition temperatures by hundreds of Kelvin. A statement bounding this uncertainty (or citing a study that has estimated it for a comparable system) would inform the reader how to interpret the 1700 K occupancy results.

---

## Recommendation

I recommend major revision. The CNAV scheme and the occupancy comparison with experiment are original and valuable contributions. However, the manuscript as currently written does not provide sufficient crystallographic information to meet the standards of a crystallography-aware TCP metallurgy paper in PRB. The two major comments — adding space groups and Pearson symbols and comparing the CEF phase stability topology with the assessed Fe–Mo phase diagram — are essential for the manuscript to be independently reproducible and physically interpretable. The minor comments are substantive but can be addressed in the text without new computations.
