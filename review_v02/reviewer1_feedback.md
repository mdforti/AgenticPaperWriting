# Referee Report — Reviewer 1 (TCP Phases & Industrial Applications)

**Manuscript:** *Data-efficient machine-learning of complex Fe--Mo intermetallics using domain knowledge of chemistry and crystallography* (Forti et al.)
**Journal:** Physical Review B

## (i) Summary

The authors present a machine-learning workflow that predicts formation energies and finite-temperature sublattice occupancies of the topologically close-packed (TCP) phases in the Fe--Mo system. They train kernel ridge regression, random forest, and MLP regressors on 262 DFT calculations of "simple" TCP phases (A15, C14, C15, C36, σ, χ, μ) and validate on 70 calculations of the structurally complex R, M, P and δ phases. The novelty resides in (i) the coordination-number-resolved averaging (CNAV) of local descriptors and (ii) the comparison of BOP, ACE and SOAP fingerprints, with BOP descriptors emerging as the most transferable. A Bragg–Williams/CEF thermodynamic step yields R-phase Mo site fractions in claimed quantitative agreement (MAD = 0.04) with the Joubert 1993 XRD refinement. From a TCP-phase and industrial-alloy perspective, the work is technically coherent and the Fe--Mo system is a sensible test bed, but the manuscript currently reads as a methods paper that is largely disconnected from the metallurgical community it implicitly addresses, and several crystallographic and experimental-comparison claims need substantiation before the paper can be considered for publication in PRB.

## (ii) Major comments

1. **Industrial motivation is asserted but not built (Introduction, p. 1, l. 2–3).** The opening sentence states only that TCP phases "commonly precipitate in high-performance steels and nickel-based superalloys during service at elevated temperatures" and that their formation "can degrade mechanical properties by consuming solid-solution strengtheners and acting as crack initiation sites." This is the entire industrial framing of the paper. For PRB this is acceptable in principle, but the manuscript later claims (Conclusions, l. 14) that the work "establishes a pathway for efficient computational discovery of TCP phase stability in multi-principal element alloys" — a claim that demands a more substantive bridge to the alloy-design literature. I would expect the authors to cite and discuss at least the canonical references: Sims, Stoloff & Hagel (*Superalloys II*), Reed (*The Superalloys*, CUP 2006), Rae & Reed (Acta Mater. 49, 4113 (2001)) on TCP precipitation in 2nd/3rd-generation single-crystal Ni-base superalloys, Sinha (Prog. Mater. Sci. 1972) on the geometry of Frank–Kasper phases, and the PHACOMP / New PHACOMP / *Md* approaches that industry actually uses. None of these appear in the bibliography. Without them, the link to "multi-principal element alloys" is rhetorical.

2. **Fe--Mo is presented as a "model system" but its own metallurgical relevance is not stated.** Fe--Mo σ, μ, and R precipitate in ferritic/martensitic 9–12 Cr power-plant steels, in maraging steels, and in Mo-bearing austenitic stainless steels (e.g. 316L, 254 SMO, duplex/super-duplex grades, where R-phase embrittlement is well documented; see Nilsson, *Mater. Sci. Technol.* 8, 685 (1992); Sieurin & Sandström, *Mater. Sci. Eng. A* 444, 271 (2007)). The authors should explicitly tie their phase selection to these service-relevant precipitates rather than to a purely structural taxonomy. As written, the choice of phases reads as opportunistic (driven by the available Crivello/Joubert dataset) rather than as a deliberate alloy-design choice.

3. **Crystallographic specification of CNAV is incomplete (Methods, "CNAV Aggregation", p. 2).** The authors group atoms "within groups sharing the same coordination number (CN)" with a 3.5 Å first-shell cutoff inferred from "ACE radial function analysis", and state that "the number of CN groups varying between 3 and 6 depending on the phase." For TCP phases the meaningful classification is the Frank–Kasper polyhedral classification (CN12 icosahedron, CN14, CN15, CN16) — this is canonical (Frank & Kasper, Acta Cryst. 11, 184 (1958); 12, 483 (1959)). The manuscript never demonstrates that the geometric CN obtained from a 3.5 Å cutoff actually reproduces the Frank–Kasper classification for the R, M, P and δ phases, where small distortions can push 2nd-shell neighbours below or above an isotropic distance cutoff. A table mapping CNAV groups to Wyckoff sites and Frank–Kasper polyhedra for each phase is required. As an example, the R phase contains 11 Wyckoff sites distributed over CN12/14/15/16; collapsing this onto "3–6 CN groups" potentially throws away the very crystallographic information the method claims to preserve.

4. **The R-phase site-occupancy comparison (Table II) is over-sold.** The authors quote MAD = 0.04 and call the agreement "quantitative." Two concerns:
   (a) The Joubert (1993) refinement is, to my knowledge, performed on a sample annealed at a specific temperature (1473–1573 K, depending on the alloy) and at a specific bulk composition. The manuscript uses T = 1700 K (Methods, p. 3) "to match the typical annealing temperature of the experimental XRD measurements", which is in fact above the upper stability limit of the R phase in the assessed Fe--Mo diagram (Andersson, *CALPHAD* 12, 1 (1988); Houserová et al., *CALPHAD* 26, 513 (2002)). Please justify or correct the temperature; using a temperature outside the phase field for a comparison with experiment is a real problem.
   (b) Experimental XRD site-occupancy refinements in TCP phases carry uncertainties of order 0.02–0.05 in site fraction even for high-quality datasets because of correlations between Fe and Mo scattering factors. The authors should report the experimental uncertainty alongside their MAD; a MAD of 0.04 may be no better than the experimental error bar, in which case "quantitative agreement" is a fair claim but should be phrased as "within experimental uncertainty". A comparison with later refinements would strengthen the case (e.g. Joubert & Dupin, *Intermetallics* 12, 1373 (2004); Joubert *Prog. Mater. Sci.* 53, 528 (2008)).

5. **Wyckoff labelling of the R phase is non-standard.** Table II lists sites "3a, 6c, 6f, 18h, 18i, 18j" for the R phase. The R phase of Fe--Mo (R$\bar{3}$, hP159 or hR53 depending on setting) is conventionally described with 11 Wyckoff positions including 18g/18h, 6c, 3a, 3b, etc. (see Yakel, *Acta Cryst. B* 39, 20 (1983); Joubert 2008). The list in Table II is missing several sites and the multiplicities do not sum to the expected ~53 atoms in the rhombohedral cell. Please reconcile with the standard setting and either show the full set or justify which sites are excluded and why.

6. **Transferability claims to other systems are unsupported (Discussion, "Generality and limitations").** The text claims the methodology "is expected to generalise to other transition-metal binary systems that host TCP phases (e.g., Fe--W, Co--Mo, Ni--Cr--Mo)." No evidence is presented for any of these systems. Given that Ni--Cr--Mo and Co--Mo σ/μ/P phases are the industrially most damaging cases, this hedged statement is the only point in the manuscript that touches the headline question — and it is a one-sentence speculation. Either tone down the claim, or provide a single transfer test (even one phase in Co--Mo or Ni--Mo using existing DFT data, e.g. from Crivello, Joubert et al.). A claim of this type, made in the abstract and conclusions, should not rest on no data.

7. **Engineering payoff for CALPHAD assessors is not articulated.** The CEF/Bragg–Williams machinery is exactly the framework used in modern CALPHAD assessments of TCP phases (Joubert, Dupin, Sundman; see Lukas, Fries, Sundman *Computational Thermodynamics*, CUP 2007). The natural deliverable from this work is a set of end-member energies that can be ingested into a TDB file. The authors should at least tabulate the ML-predicted end-member energies for the R, M, P, δ phases (Supplementary at minimum), so that the CALPHAD community can use them. As it stands, the paper demonstrates capability without delivering the product.

## (iii) Minor comments

1. Abstract, "remarkable structural hierarchy" — please soften; it is structurally rich, not "remarkable".
2. Introduction, p. 1, l. 3: cite Hall & Algie (*Metall. Rev.* 11, 61 (1966)) and Sinha (1972) for the classical TCP review.
3. Methods, p. 2: "TCP phases are non-magnetic" — true for Fe--Mo TCP at the compositions studied, but the σ phase in Fe--Cr and Fe--V is weakly ferromagnetic. Please qualify.
4. Table I "Convergence" — the differences shown are 0.0–0.3 meV/at, smaller than the EOS fitting noise. Either combine with an EOS-residual estimate or move to Supplementary.
5. Figure 1 caption: define "filled" vs "open" symbols (currently both train and test are described identically).
6. Table II: please give XRD uncertainties σ(y_Mo) per site.
7. Bibliography is incomplete: only ~20 entries shown; for a PRB submission on a metallurgically loaded topic this is too sparse. In particular, no entries by Sims, Reed, Rae, Sinha, Frank, Kasper, Yakel, Nilsson, Andersson, Houserová, or Hammerschmidt & Drautz on BOP for TCP stability (Hammerschmidt et al., *Adv. Eng. Mater.* 10, 1115 (2008); *Phys. Rev. B* 87, 014203 (2013)).
8. The term "complex TCP phase" is used loosely; please define operationally (e.g. ≥10 Wyckoff sites) on first use.
9. "$\sim$80, $\sim$200, $\sim$100" selected features in Table I are approximate — please give exact numbers per fold or median±IQR.
10. Section ordering: PRB Regular Articles normally place Methods before Results; consider checking journal preference (the current Results → Discussion → Methods → Conclusions order reads as a Nature-style legacy from the npj draft).

## (iv) Recommendation

**Major revision.**
The science is sound and the Fe--Mo TCP test bed is well chosen, but the manuscript needs substantial strengthening of its crystallographic specification (CNAV ↔ Frank–Kasper mapping, correct R-phase Wyckoff list), its experimental comparison (temperature, uncertainties, later refinements), and its connection to the alloy-design and CALPHAD communities it implicitly addresses, before it meets PRB's bar for impact and rigor.
