\subsection{DFT Calculations}

All density-functional theory (DFT) calculations were performed using the Vienna Ab initio Simulation Package (VASP~\cite{vasp}) with the projector augmented wave (PAW) method~\cite{paw} and the Perdew--Burke--Ernzerhof (PBE) exchange-correlation functional~\cite{pbe}. The plane-wave energy cutoff was fixed at 450~eV for all calculations to avoid Pulay stress variations across the $E$--$V$ curve. Monkhorst--Pack $k$-point meshes~\cite{monkhorst_pack} were chosen to ensure total energy convergence to within 1~\meVat. For TCP phases with large unit cells (R, M, P, $\delta$), single $\Gamma$-only calculations were used, while denser meshes were employed for the smaller simple TCP phases. Table~\ref{tab:convergence} demonstrates that $\Gamma$-only sampling is sufficient for the large-unit-cell phases: formation energies change by less than 0.5~\meVat when using a $2\times2\times2$ mesh.

\begin{table}[htbp]
  \centering
  \caption{Convergence of formation energy with $k$-point mesh for representative complex TCP structures.}
  \label{tab:convergence}
  \begin{tabular}{lccc}
    \toprule
    Phase & $\Gamma$-only & $2\times2\times2$ & Difference (\meVat) \\
    \midrule
    R  & 0.000 & 0.000 & --- \\
    M  & 0.023 & 0.023 & $<$0.1 \\
    P  & $-0.045$ & $-0.045$ & $<$0.1 \\
    $\delta$ & 0.112 & 0.111 & 0.3 \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

For each structure, we performed a series of constant-volume calculations spanning a range of volumes around the equilibrium, followed by $E$--$V$ curve fitting using a third-order Birch--Murnaghan equation of state (EOS)~\cite{birch_murnaghan} to obtain the equilibrium volume $V_0$, bulk modulus $B_0$, and the equilibrium formation energy. This procedure eliminates Pulay stress errors arising from basis-set incompleteness at different volumes, since the energy cutoff is held constant across all $E$--$V$ points.

The target property for machine learning was the formation energy referenced to non-magnetic (NM) hcp Fe and hcp Mo, denoted $E_f^{nmhcp}$. TCP phases are non-magnetic, so the NM reference avoids spurious magnetic contributions that would arise from a ferromagnetic bcc Fe reference. The magnetic energy of bcc Fe relative to NM hcp Fe is approximately 0.5~eV/atom, which shifts absolute formation energies but does not affect the relative energy differences between TCP polymorphs that are the focus of this work.

\subsection{Descriptor Computation}

\subsubsection*{BOP Moments}

Bond-order potential (BOP) moments were computed using the BOPfox code (v2020)~\cite{bopfox}. We employed a canonical $d$-band tight-binding Hamiltonian with a rectangular $d$-band model; tests with an elliptic band shape gave moment values within 3\% of the rectangular case. For each atom, the first four moments of the local density of states ($\mu_0$, $\mu_1$, $\mu_2$, $\mu_3$) were computed from the crystal structure. The fourth moment already captures bond-angle sensitivity sufficient to distinguish TCP polytypes; tests including $\mu_4$ and $\mu_5$ changed RMSE by less than 2~\meVat. Two projection schemes were used: (i) \textbf{canonical}---moments from the pure tight-binding Hamiltonian, yielding 405-dimensional feature vectors after CNAV; and (ii) \textbf{0.7dProjections 0.5OS}---moments projected onto DFT-calculated local densities of states using a scissor operator (shift 0.7~eV, smearing 0.5~eV), yielding 417-dimensional feature vectors. The latter gave consistently better performance.

\subsubsection*{ACE Descriptors}

Atomic cluster expansion (ACE) descriptors~\cite{ace} were computed using the \texttt{python-ace} package. The ACE framework provides a complete linear basis of rotationally invariant many-body functions of the local atomic environment. We used a cutoff radius of \SI{6.0}{\textnormal{~\AA}}, correlation orders 2--4, and radial basis functions spaced at \SI{0.5}{\textnormal{~\AA}} intervals. The full ACE basis yielded up to 1803 features per structure. A ``canonical'' variant using only the dominant radial basis functions reduced this to 417 features while retaining most of the predictive accuracy.

\subsubsection*{SOAP Descriptors}

Smooth overlap of atomic positions (SOAP) descriptors~\cite{soap} were computed using the DScribe library (v2.0)~\cite{dsribe}. The SOAP power spectrum was calculated with: $r_\text{cut} = \SI{6.0}{\textnormal{~\AA}}$, $n_\text{max} = 8$ radial basis functions, and $l_\text{max} = 6$ spherical harmonics. Element-specific SOAP vectors (Fe--Fe, Fe--Mo, Mo--Mo, Mo--Fe) were computed for each central atom and concatenated. The resulting per-atom SOAP vectors had dimension~287 after CNAV aggregation.

\subsubsection*{CNAV Aggregation}

For each structure, the per-atom descriptor vectors were averaged within groups sharing the same coordination number (CN). The CN for each atom was determined from the ACE radial function analysis using a cutoff of \SI{3.5}{\textnormal{~\AA}} for the first shell. The CN-resolved averages were concatenated into a single structure-level fingerprint, with the number of CN groups varying between 3 and 6 depending on the phase.

CNAV preserves crystallographic information by treating each CN environment separately, while reducing noise from per-atom fluctuations. To assess what information is preserved versus lost, we compared CNAV with per-site descriptors (no averaging): CNAV improves RMSE by 20--40\% over conventional per-structure averaging and by approximately 10\% over per-site descriptors, confirming that CN-grouping captures the essential crystallographic distinctions between phases without discarding discriminatory power.

\subsection{Model Training and Evaluation}

All machine learning models were implemented using scikit-learn (v1.2)~\cite{sklearn}. The training/test split (209/53) was stratified by phase to ensure that each simple TCP phase was represented in both sets. Features were standardised to zero mean and unit variance using the training set statistics. Random seeds were fixed for reproducibility: \texttt{numpy.random.seed(42)}, \texttt{sklearn.utils.check\_random\_state(42)}, and \texttt{random.seed(42)}.

\subsubsection*{Model Selection}

Three regression algorithms were considered:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Kernel Ridge Regression (KRR):} RBF kernel with $\alpha \in [10^{-4}, 10^{-2}]$ and $\gamma \in [10^{-4}, 10^{-1}]$ optimised via grid search with 5-fold cross-validation (CV).
  \item \textbf{Random Forest (RF):} 200 trees, $\text{max\_depth} \in [10, 50]$ and $\text{min\_samples\_leaf} \in [1, 10]$ optimised via grid search with 5-fold CV.
  \item \textbf{Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP):} Single hidden layer with 64 neurons, ReLU activation, trained with Adam optimiser (learning rate $10^{-3}$) for up to 1000 epochs with early stopping. Regularisation parameter $\alpha \in [10^{-4}, 10^{-2}]$ was optimised via 5-fold CV.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection*{Ensemble Construction}

For each feature set, a VotingRegressor ensemble was constructed by averaging the predictions of the three optimally tuned models (KRR, RF, MLP). This approach consistently outperformed individual models, with the ensemble RMSE being 5--15\% lower than the best individual model.

\subsubsection*{Feature Selection}

Forward recursive feature selection was performed separately for each model/feature combination, nested inside the 5-fold CV loop: for each fold, selection was applied to the training portion only. Starting from the full feature set, features were added one at a time based on the improvement in 5-fold CV RMSE. Selection was terminated when the CV RMSE stopped improving by more than 0.5~\meVat. This procedure reduced the dimensionality by factors of 2--10 depending on the feature set (from $\sim$400--1800 to $\sim$50--300). Feature selection was embarrassingly parallel and was executed on the cluster using 16 cores per job. The final optimised hyperparameters for each model/feature combination are reported in Supplementary Table~S2.

\subsubsection*{Evaluation Metrics}

Model performance was evaluated using:
\begin{align}
  \text{RMSE} &= \sqrt{\frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} (y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2}, \\
  \text{MAE} &= \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} |y_i - \hat{y}_i|.
\end{align}
Both metrics were computed on the test set (53 structures) and the independent validation set (70 structures).

\subsection{Thermodynamic Modelling}

Finite-temperature thermodynamic properties were computed within the compound energy formalism (CEF) using the Bragg--Williams (BW) approximation, as implemented in the \texttt{pycef} code. The Gibbs free energy of each phase is given by:

\begin{equation}
  G = \sum_i y_i G_i^\circ + RT \sum_s \sum_i y_i^{(s)} \ln y_i^{(s)} + G^E
  \label{eq:gibbs}
\end{equation}

where $y_i^{(s)}$ is the site fraction of species $i$ on sublattice $s$, $G_i^\circ$ is the ML-predicted formation energy of the end-member compound, and $G^E$ is the excess Gibbs free energy. The ideal BW approximation ($G^E = 0$) was used as a first approximation, neglecting short-range order (SRO). To test the sensitivity to this assumption, we performed calculations with small excess terms $L = \pm 5$ and $\pm 10$~kJ/mol. The R-phase site occupancies changed by less than 0.03 in site fraction for all but the mixed 6c~site ($\Delta y_{\text{Mo}} \sim 0.08$), justifying the ideal BW approximation at the accuracy level of this study (Supplementary Figure~S2). The temperature of 1700~K was chosen to match the typical annealing temperature of the experimental XRD measurements~\cite{joubert_fe_mo} and lies within the stability range of all studied TCP phases. Sublattice occupancies were obtained by minimising Eq.~\eqref{eq:gibbs} with respect to site fractions using a sequential least-squares programming (SLSQP) optimiser~\cite{slsqp} from 10 random starting points; the same global minimum was found in all cases.
